Uncertainty and Probability

🧪 OBJECTIVE

By the end of the lesson, the student will be able to explain the term uncertainty in relation to quantum science.

RPK: The student can perform simple algebraic operations, construct a histogram from frequency tables, solve basic probability exercises (demonstrate with a coin toss).

✅ MATERIALS

  • Cubes (Dice)
  • Graph sheets
  • Worksheets

📝 ACTIVITIES

  1. Using questions and answers, find out what the student already knows from the RPK.
  2. Let the students brainstorm to give their understanding of "Uncertainty".
  3. Let the students attempt to classify the outcome of a few physical events as "certain" or "uncertain".
  4. Revise the concept of Standard deviation and its role in the Uncertainty principle.
  5. In groups, guide the students to set up two dice with different colored dots, numbered 1 to 6.
    • Ask the students to roll the two Dice 14 times and record the sum of each roll in Observation Table A.
    • Have a discussion based on the following:
      (α) Mean
      (β) Most probable (mode)
  7. Guide learners to complete the "Observation Table B".
  8. Guide learners to construct a frequency table from the "Observation Table B" and discuss it. Name this table "Observation Table C".
  9. Guide the students to plot a histogram from the frequency table using their graph sheets and let them discuss their observations.
  10. Guide the students to quantify the Uncertainty in terms of Standard deviation using the histogram.
  11. Explain the results and discuss the significance of the activities and the results.

📊 OBSERVATION TABLE A

Roll two dice 14 times and record their sum in the spaces provided below.

  1. Write down your observation(s) from the table A above.
    The sums are not equally likely; some (like 7) appear more often. The distribution is not uniform.
  2. What do you think will happen if you continue to roll the two dice a large number of times (100)?
    The frequency of each sum will approach the theoretical probability. The most common sum will be 7, and the distribution will form a bell shape.

📋 OBSERVATION TABLE B

Without rolling the two dice this time, complete the table by filling the blank spaces with possible outcomes of sums when two dice are rolled.

  1. Write down your observation from the table B.
    There are more ways to get some sums (like 7) than others (like 2 or 12). The number of ways increases to 7, then decreases.

📈 OBSERVATION TABLE C

Construct a frequency table from the Observation Table B above.

  1. Write down your observation from Table C.
    The frequency table shows a symmetric distribution centered at 7. The probability of each sum matches the number of ways to roll it.

📉 HISTOGRAM

Draw a HISTOGRAM for Table C on a graph sheet.

  1. Describe your observation on the histogram.
    The histogram forms a bell-shaped curve, peaking at 7. This shows the most probable outcome is 7, and the distribution is not uniform.

📝 FOOTNOTE: Quantum Uncertainty

In quantum physics: Particles like electrons don't have a single definite position or momentum before measurement. Instead, they're described by a wavefunction, which gives a probability distribution for each observable. When we say there's "uncertainty" in position or momentum, we mean the spread (standard deviation) of the probability distribution of outcomes if we measured many times.

The Key Insight: Quantum uncertainty is not due to randomness in measurement or instruments — it's due to the fundamental probabilistic nature of the system itself.

  • In classical statistics, uncertainty reflects lack of knowledge (e.g., we don't know which die face will land).
  • In quantum mechanics, uncertainty reflects inherent indeterminacy — the outcome doesn't exist in a definite state until it is measured.

  1. What type of pattern appears when two slits are open and photons or electrons are used?
    An interference pattern of alternating bright and dark fringes appears, showing wave-like behavior.
  2. What surprising thing happens when you send electrons one at a time?
    Even when sent one at a time, electrons build up the same interference pattern over time, suggesting each electron interferes with itself.

    The appearance of an interference pattern (fringes) even for single electrons shows wave-like behavior.

Photoelectric Effect

🧪 OBJECTIVE

To investigate how light can knock electrons out of a metal surface (the photoelectric effect), and explore how this supports the idea that light acts like particles (photons).

RPK: Learners should know about light, intensity of light, the relationship frequency and wavelength of a wave.

✅ MATERIALS

  • PhET Photoelectric Effect simulation

🚀 PART 1: Setup and Controls

  1. Open the simulation.
  2. You'll see:
    • A light source (wavelength and intensity sliders)
    • A metal surface
    • A vacuum chamber and ammeter to measure electron flow
    • A detector

PART 2: Explore Photon Energy (Wavelength)

Keep intensity constant (25%). Change wavelength (color) of the light.

🧠 CONCLUSION QUESTIONS

  1. Does changing the intensity of low-energy light (e.g., red) cause photoelectrons to be ejected?
    No, increasing intensity of low-energy (low-frequency) light does not cause photoelectrons to be ejected if the photon energy is below the threshold.
  2. Does increasing the frequency (or using shorter wavelengths) cause photoelectron ejection for a given material e.g. Na?
    Yes, increasing the frequency (shorter wavelength) above the threshold causes photoelectron ejection for that material.
  3. How does the number of photons (intensity) affect the number of photoelectrons?
    Increasing intensity (number of photons) increases the number of photoelectrons ejected, but only if the photon energy is above the threshold.
  4. What happens if each photon's energy is below the threshold?
    No photoelectrons are ejected, regardless of the intensity of the light.
  5. Based on what you observed, why must light behave like particles (photons) in this experiment?
    Because only photons with enough energy (above threshold frequency) can eject electrons, showing that energy is delivered in discrete packets (quanta), not continuously as a wave.

🔑 Key Concept: Why This Proves Light Is a Particle

The photoelectric effect doesn't work like a wave:

  • Increasing brightness (intensity) doesn't help if the frequency is too low.
  • Only high-frequency photons (each with enough energy) can eject electrons.
  • This means energy is delivered in chunks (quanta) — proving light is made of particles!
